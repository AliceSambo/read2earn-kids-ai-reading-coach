# Read2Earn Kids Visual Product Bible

## Purpose

This Product Bible is the creative blueprint for the premium Read2Earn Kids experience. It translates the approved product principles, design system, companion framework, and Living Reading Mission into one coherent visual world.

It does not redesign or replace the current prototype. It defines the destination that future implementation phases should approach in safe, testable slices.

The north-star promise is:

> Every book opens a world. Every act of understanding helps that world grow.

## 1. World vision

### The emotional experience of entering

Opening Read2Earn Kids should feel like arriving at a familiar doorway to a place that remembers how learning feels—not a dashboard, content catalogue, arcade, or classroom management system.

The first emotional sequence is:

1. **Welcome:** “This place is safe and made for me.”
2. **Choice:** “I can choose how I enter and who comes with me.”
3. **Wonder:** “There are worlds here that respond to what I discover.”
4. **Agency:** “I can read independently or ask for support.”
5. **Capability:** “My explanation changed something because I understood.”
6. **Connection:** “A grown-up can see what I learned and help me continue.”

The visual atmosphere is warm, luminous, layered, and calm. Mystery comes from discovery, never darkness, danger, urgency, scarcity, or fear. The world invites attention without demanding constant engagement.

### How books become missions

A book is not presented as a file in a grid. It becomes a **Living Reading Mission** with five connected layers:

- **Place:** the world or location that gives the story context.
- **Purpose:** a child-friendly reason to read, such as helping a character or discovering how something works.
- **Reading:** the story remains central, with optional companion, narration, vocabulary, and pronunciation support.
- **Understanding:** the child explains meaning in their own words through typed or supported voice input.
- **Transformation:** demonstrated evidence changes one meaningful part of the world and earns a named Knowledge Gem.

Mission language must remain truthful. The interface never claims that opening pages, spending time, or tapping quickly proves learning.

### How learning changes the world

World changes make evidence visible. Each transformation corresponds to a demonstrated skill:

- a safe path lights after the child explains a helping action and outcome;
- a machine activates after the child identifies a cause-and-effect relationship;
- a bridge completes after the child explains a mathematical strategy;
- a village sign becomes readable after vocabulary or language understanding;
- a map reveals a route after the child connects facts about a place;
- a school garden grows after a verified class learning goal is met in a future adult-controlled school space.

Transformations are:

- meaningful rather than decorative;
- local to the mission rather than global pressure;
- permanent only when appropriate and recoverable in the prototype;
- described in text as well as shown visually;
- replaced with a clear static before/after state in reduced-motion mode.

### The connected learning relationship

#### The child

The child is the reader, decision-maker, and meaning-maker. They choose the reading mode, ask for help, explain understanding, and decide how much they enjoyed the story. They are never treated as a data point, rank, customer, or content-consumption target.

#### The companion

The companion makes support feel natural. It notices, listens, offers one useful clue, models calm strategy, and celebrates effort separately from demonstrated understanding. It recedes during sustained reading and never pretends to be a real friend, teacher, or grown-up.

#### The story

The story is the source of meaning. Visual effects, AI assistance, rewards, and companion dialogue remain subordinate to the text and learning goal. The story is never reduced to a backdrop for points.

#### The parent or grown-up

The grown-up confirms sensitive setup choices, controls privacy and narration preferences, and receives calm evidence: what the child understood, which help they used, words explored, and one useful next activity. The grown-up view explains rather than surveils.

Together, these form a trusted loop:

```text
Child chooses → companion supports → story invites meaning
       → child demonstrates understanding → world responds
       → grown-up receives evidence and a next step
```

## 2. World map architecture

### Shared map rules

The learning world is a stable geography, not an endlessly changing promotion surface. Each destination has a distinct silhouette, palette emphasis, sound identity when enabled, learning purpose, and low-motion list equivalent.

Worlds are equally valuable. Availability is communicated plainly; unavailable areas do not pulse, tease, count down, or imply scarcity. A child’s reading level changes support, not which world they are “allowed” to deserve.

### Story Forest

**Purpose:** Build reading fluency, vocabulary, comprehension, empathy, moral reasoning, and story structure through narrative missions.

**Visual mood:** Layered woodland at golden dusk; paper-cut foliage; lanterns; winding paths; warm parchment story clearings; Journey Violet shadows and Canopy Teal leaves.

**Learning categories:** Fiction, folktales, faith and character stories, moral stories, narrative nonfiction, sequencing, main idea, character motivation, and vocabulary in context.

**Future expansion:** Seasonal story trails without scarcity pressure; author-curated groves; culturally specific storytelling regions; accessible audio-described illustrations; family read-together clearings; school-assigned trails controlled by adults.

### Discovery Lab

**Purpose:** Turn science and inquiry into observable missions where children predict, investigate, explain, and connect cause to effect.

**Visual mood:** A bright greenhouse laboratory built from glass, wood, plants, lenses, and friendly mechanical forms. Discovery Blue and Canopy Teal lead; Companion Coral marks interactive hypotheses.

**Learning categories:** Life science, physical science, earth science, health, technology, observation, classification, prediction, and evidence-based explanation.

**Future expansion:** Safe virtual experiments; local-environment missions; downloadable observation sheets; teacher-authored inquiry prompts; accessibility-first diagrams; explicit separation between simulation and real-world fact.

### Number Kingdom

**Purpose:** Make mathematical thinking visible through strategy, pattern, quantity, shape, and problem-solving missions.

**Visual mood:** A welcoming geometric kingdom of bridges, gardens, towers, tiles, and waterways. Forms align, rotate, group, and balance. Journey Violet anchors navigation while Learning Gold marks solved relationships, not speed.

**Learning categories:** Number sense, operations, fractions, measurement, geometry, patterns, data, logical reasoning, and explaining a strategy.

**Future expansion:** Manipulative-based puzzles; multiple valid strategies; adaptive scaffolds that do not label ability; curriculum-aligned mission paths; teacher-created challenges without public ranking or time pressure.

### Language Village

**Purpose:** Build confidence in vocabulary, grammar, expression, listening, pronunciation, and additional languages through meaningful communication.

**Visual mood:** A lively but uncluttered village of signs, markets, story stages, gardens, and conversation corners. Warm Parchment surfaces, Companion Coral accents, and Discovery Blue wayfinding create friendliness without visual noise.

**Learning categories:** English language, French and future languages, spelling, sentence building, pronunciation, listening comprehension, expressive writing, and context-sensitive vocabulary.

**Future expansion:** Dialect-aware pronunciation support reviewed for fairness; bilingual stories; culturally grounded language packs; role-play with bounded scripted characters rather than open child messaging; teacher-reviewed writing prompts.

### World Explorer

**Purpose:** Connect children with geography, culture, history, agriculture, environment, and civic understanding through respectful place-based missions.

**Visual mood:** A navigable atlas of rivers, farms, coastlines, cities, skies, and cultural landmarks rendered as an illustrated globe and travel journal. Earth tones, Discovery Blue water, and Learning Gold route markers create a sense of wonder without exoticizing people or places.

**Learning categories:** Geography, history, cultures, agriculture, environment, citizenship, map skills, timelines, comparison, and source awareness.

**Future expansion:** Regional content partnerships; local-history collections; climate and sustainability missions; primary-source galleries; adult-reviewed field activities; downloadable low-bandwidth maps; content-rights and cultural-sensitivity review.

### School Island

**Purpose:** Provide a future adult-controlled bridge between the child’s learning identity and a verified institution’s private books, lessons, assignments, and evidence.

**Visual mood:** A calm campus island with a library, workshop, garden, classrooms, and assembly space. Adult Canvas structures meet the child-world illustration language. It feels connected to the map but visibly governed by grown-ups.

**Learning categories:** School-assigned reading, curriculum subjects, teacher lessons, private textbooks, formative assessment, class-specific learning evidence, and approved competitions without public child profiles.

**Future expansion:** Verified school membership; class libraries; teacher assignments; continuity during absence; offline access; institution reports; paid exams or events only with explicit adult and school controls. None of these capabilities belong to the current prototype.

### Naming transition note

The current prototype labels future destinations differently in places, including Discovery Ocean and Character Garden. The Product Bible defines the long-term information architecture. Any future rename must be implemented as a reviewed UI migration with updated content, semantics, tests, and accessibility labels—not as an undocumented text replacement.

## 3. Companion visual bible

### Final art direction

The companion system uses **storybook explorers**: original, species-inspired creatures with simple silhouettes, expressive faces, and one small learning tool such as a lantern, lens, notebook, or satchel. They belong naturally in every world without appearing to own or lead it.

The two approved concept families remain:

- **Trail Guide:** grounded, observant, steady, and nature-oriented.
- **Spark Inventor:** bright, strategic, expressive, and inquiry-oriented.

These are art and behavior directions, not names. The child chooses an available appearance and supplies the companion’s name. A grown-up confirms the appearance, custom name, and voice preference before entry.

### Illustration style

- Two-dimensional digital storybook art with layered paper, gouache-like texture, and clean vector-readable edges.
- Shapes stay simple at navigation size and gain texture only at larger sizes.
- Lighting is soft and directional; the companion never glows more strongly than evidence or the primary action.
- Outlines use Deep Story Ink or a harmonized dark local color rather than pure black.
- Accessories communicate function, never rarity, wealth, gender, or status.
- All production assets require documented ownership, source files, export history, and approved usage rights.

### Proportions

Use a compact, child-readable proportion system:

- head approximately 40–45% of total standing height for expression clarity;
- eyes large enough to communicate gaze at 48px, without baby-like exaggeration;
- hands, paws, fins, or wings large enough to point clearly;
- short stable limbs and a low center of gravity for safety and calm;
- accessory volume below 15% of the silhouette so recognition does not depend on it.

Every silhouette must remain distinguishable at 48px, in monochrome, and under common forms of color-vision deficiency.

### Facial expression set

Each companion requires the same neutral expression vocabulary:

| Expression | Visual cue | Product use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Calm ready | relaxed brows, open gaze | waiting for a choice |
| Curious | slight head angle, focused eyes | noticing a clue or word |
| Listening | steady gaze, quiet mouth | child voice or reflection |
| Thinking | eyes shift toward clue, soft brow | bounded processing state |
| Encouraging | warm eyes, small smile | effort and retry |
| Delighted | wider smile, lifted posture | evidence-backed celebration |
| Concerned, never alarmed | softened brow, neutral mouth | unavailable support or recoverable issue |
| Resting | lowered energy, peaceful posture | reading and natural stopping point |

There is no angry, disappointed, crying, frightened, impatient, or judgmental child-feedback expression.

### Animation states

| State | Full-motion behavior | Reduced-motion equivalent |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ready | one slow breath or blink, then still | static calm pose |
| Selected | brief acknowledgment turn | outline and expression change |
| Listening | still pose with a small captioned indicator | static listening icon and text |
| Thinking | one short tool or spark sequence | static thinking symbol and status text |
| Helping | points once toward the relevant element | pointer line or highlight appears |
| Encouraging | small nod or hand-to-heart gesture | expression and supportive text change |
| Celebrating | one hop, wing lift, or spark arc | evidence card and final pose appear |
| Resting | seated or tucked posture | static resting illustration |

Animation never loops beside story text, blocks a control, covers an error, or lasts long enough to delay the next action. Audio is never triggered by animation alone.

### Accessibility requirements

- Companion actions have text equivalents and do not carry essential meaning alone.
- Gaze and pointing never replace focus, labels, instructions, or reading order.
- Expression meaning is reinforced through language, shape, and iconography—not color alone.
- High-contrast, reduced-detail, and reduced-motion export variants are required.
- Captions accompany spoken companion guidance.
- Voice controls include preview, quiet mode, speed, and safe fallback.
- The companion does not move continuously in the reading measure.
- Decorative companion images use empty alternative text; informative states receive concise contextual labels.

### Emotional boundaries

The companion may say:

- “Let’s look for one clue together.”
- “You found part of what happened.”
- “Would you like to hear that word?”

The companion may not:

- imply exclusive friendship, dependency, or human consciousness;
- say the child disappointed it;
- demand return visits or mention streak loss;
- ask for secrets, full names, location, school identity, contact details, or private conversation;
- compare one child’s progress with another;
- promise a Knowledge Gem before evidence exists;
- diagnose reading ability, emotion, disability, or age;
- replace adult, teacher, literacy, or safeguarding guidance.

## 4. Golden path UX

The Golden Path is the shortest complete experience that communicates the product promise. Each screen has one emotional job and one dominant action.

### Flagship screen 1 — Welcome

**Purpose:** Establish the living-world promise, trust boundary, and first action.

**User action:** Begin the adventure, preview grown-up evidence, or open comfort settings.

**Emotional goal:** Safe curiosity—“I know what this is, and I want to see what is beyond the path.”

**Visual direction:** A storybook threshold with a distant world map, one dim Story Forest lantern, a restrained companion silhouette, and a high-contrast Journey Violet invitation. Privacy reassurance sits near the action without becoming legalistic.

**Accessibility requirements:** Skip link; one `h1`; logical focus order; 48px targets; 200% text scaling; reduced-motion hero; no autoplay; visible privacy statement; grown-up preview identified clearly.

### Flagship screen 2 — Companion Creation

**Purpose:** Give the child safe identity and support agency before the journey.

**User action:** Choose an appearance, enter a nickname, name the companion, and preview voice preferences before grown-up review.

**Emotional goal:** Ownership without exposure—“This guide feels like mine, but I do not need to share private information.”

**Visual direction:** A quiet studio or clearing with two to four equally weighted companion cards, a central selected preview, and short fields presented after appearance selection. No character is rare, premium, smarter, or preselected as “best.”

**Accessibility requirements:** Native radio semantics; appearance descriptions; non-color selection; labelled fields; local normalized whole-word screening; clear statement that screening is basic; keyboard support; text voice alternatives; adult correction path.

### Flagship screen 3 — Learning World Map

**Purpose:** Make the breadth of learning understandable while keeping the next mission obvious.

**User action:** Enter Story Forest, inspect a world, switch to list view, or open the grown-up report boundary.

**Emotional goal:** Expansive but not overwhelming—“There is more to discover, and I know where to begin.”

**Visual direction:** A stable illustrated geography with six distinct silhouettes connected by calm paths. Story Forest receives the clear available state. Future worlds are visible with honest status text, not locks designed to create desire.

**Accessibility requirements:** Fully equivalent list view; DOM order matches reading order; no drag-only map; text names and statuses; keyboard spatial behavior documented; reduced-motion state; no color-only availability; no inaccessible map labels.

### Flagship screen 4 — Reading Mission

**Purpose:** Keep the story central while making support easy to request and understand.

**User action:** Choose a reading mode, read or listen, open vocabulary or pronunciation help, move between parts, and explain the story.

**Emotional goal:** Capable concentration—“I can do this, and help is available without interrupting me.”

**Visual direction:** Warm Parchment reading canvas, comfortable measure, restrained environment art at story beats, stable navigation, and a companion placed outside the core text column. Vocabulary words are discoverable without making the paragraph look like a game board.

**Accessibility requirements:** Scalable Atkinson Hyperlegible reading text; clear progress text; keyboard vocabulary actions; narration control and quiet mode; typed voice fallback; no forced timing; no motion near current text; focus return from helpers; semantic status messages.

### Flagship screen 5 — Knowledge Gem Reward

**Purpose:** Connect demonstrated understanding to a meaningful story-world transformation and adult-readable evidence.

**User action:** Review the named skill and evidence, rate enjoyment separately, save the mission, and continue to the grown-up report.

**Emotional goal:** Earned pride—“The world changed because I explained what I understood.”

**Visual direction:** One transformation completes the mission scene; then the Knowledge Gem appears with its skill name and evidence sentence. Learning Gold is used sparingly. Enjoyment faces sit in a separate section so preference is never confused with comprehension.

**Accessibility requirements:** Skill and evidence announced in text; no color or animation-only meaning; brief non-blocking celebration; reduced-motion before/after state; labelled rating choices; immediate continue action; no confetti loop, rank, rarity, social prompt, or upsell.

## 5. Reading experience architecture

### Story page

The story page is a reading surface first. It contains:

- story title and current part;
- readable text in a constrained measure;
- optional illustration tied to the current story beat;
- visible “Part X of Y” progress;
- reading-mode label;
- narration and comfort controls;
- discoverable vocabulary actions;
- stable previous/next navigation;
- a companion support area outside the primary reading measure.

The layout does not rearrange when narration starts, help fails, or the child revises an answer.

### AI help interaction

AI, when approved and configured, is a bounded enhancement behind the server. It can:

- rephrase an approved clue in level-appropriate language;
- explain a locally defined vocabulary concept with constrained output;
- provide warm feedback after deterministic evidence is checked;
- help summarize evidence for a grown-up without changing the authoritative result.

AI cannot:

- award or remove a Knowledge Gem;
- override the deterministic character/action/outcome evidence gate;
- make an unsupported diagnosis or infer age, emotion, disability, or identity;
- ask open-ended personal questions;
- expose a key to the client;
- remove the complete local fallback;
- store voice, child profile, or conversation history in this prototype.

The UI labels unavailable enhanced help as a recoverable support limitation, never as child failure.

### Vocabulary support

Vocabulary help follows the sequence:

1. Child activates a highlighted word.
2. A helper shows the word, syllables, and a child-friendly contextual meaning.
3. Pronunciation is offered as an explicit action.
4. The child can repeat, close, or return to the exact word.
5. The report may record that the word was explored, not claim mastery.

Future examples, morphology, translation, or imagery must be content-reviewed and never crowd the first explanation.

### Pronunciation support

- Uses a clear “Hear this word” action; never autoplays.
- Shows written syllables and retains the typed/visual learning path.
- Provides visible unsupported-browser and quiet-mode messages.
- If future speech assessment is introduced, it requires verified grown-up consent, dialect and bias testing, disclosure of processing, deletion controls, and a non-recording alternative.
- Pronunciation feedback is gentle and specific; it does not grade accents against one narrow voice standard.

### Comprehension mission

The child retells meaning through typing or supported speech. The deterministic gate requires three semantic categories appropriate to the sample story:

- who needed help;
- what the main character did;
- what changed as a result.

A weak fragment receives one warm clue and an editable retry. A complete meaningful retelling reveals evidence. The system checks meaning rather than exact wording. Voice is optional, initiated only by the child, and always has a typed alternative.

### Reward evidence

The evidence object should remain conceptually separate from presentation:

```text
Skill: Comprehension
Evidence categories: character, action, outcome
Child-facing explanation: what the child noticed
Support used: reading mode, vocabulary, optional narration
World change: the story-specific transformation
Adult next step: one practical conversation or activity
```

The Knowledge Gem presentation consumes this evidence; it never manufactures it. Enjoyment rating, reading time, page completion, and animation interaction do not alter the evidence result.

## 6. Asset production plan

### Production principles

- Build assets from a versioned source library with ownership and rights metadata.
- Export by semantic name, not screen coordinates.
- Produce responsive, high-contrast, reduced-detail, and reduced-motion variants where needed.
- Keep raster texture separate from vector silhouettes so quality scales.
- Test real assets at 320px, 375px, 768px, desktop, and 200% text scaling.
- Avoid embedding text in images.

### Character assets

#### Base character packages

- Trail Guide base model and silhouette sheet.
- Spark Inventor base model and silhouette sheet.
- Palette and contrast variants.
- Monochrome and 48px recognition tests.
- Accessory layers separated from the base body.

#### Required poses

- neutral standing;
- ready or welcoming;
- seated/resting;
- listening;
- thinking;
- pointing/helping left and right;
- reading beside the child;
- holding a lantern, lens, or notebook;
- encouraging nod;
- evidence-backed celebration;
- quiet-mode and unavailable-support pose.

#### Required emotions and expressions

- calm;
- curious;
- attentive;
- thoughtful;
- encouraging;
- delighted;
- gently concerned;
- resting.

Each expression needs a static master, reduced-detail export, and concise usage note.

#### Animation deliverables

- state machine diagram;
- rig and source file;
- ready, select, listen, think, help, encourage, celebrate, and rest clips;
- maximum duration and loop policy per clip;
- reduced-motion replacement frame;
- transparent-background web export;
- performance budget and fallback image.

### Environment assets

#### World map

- complete six-world map master;
- mobile, tablet, desktop, and list-view compositions;
- world silhouettes and labels;
- available, future, focused, completed, and restored states;
- path layers and low-motion alternatives;
- high-contrast landmark markers.

#### World backgrounds

- Story Forest entrance, mission clearing, reading backdrop, dim lantern, restored path;
- Discovery Lab exterior, greenhouse, experiment bench, observation garden;
- Number Kingdom overview, bridge, pattern garden, geometry courtyard;
- Language Village overview, sign street, story stage, conversation garden;
- World Explorer globe, journal desk, farm, river, city, and timeline locations;
- School Island exterior, library, workshop, classroom, garden, and adult-control boundary.

#### Mission locations

Each flagship mission requires:

- establishing view;
- problem state;
- two or three story-beat variations;
- evidence-restored state;
- reduced-motion before/after pair;
- decorative and informative layer map;
- alternative-text guidance.

### UI assets

#### Icons

- home, back, close, settings, grown-up, privacy;
- read, listen, companion, narration, quiet, speed;
- vocabulary, syllables, pronunciation, voice, type;
- evidence, information, warning, error, success;
- map/list view, available/future/completed states;
- print and local-device storage.

Every icon needs a 24px and 32px optical version and must work with a text label.

#### Knowledge Gems

- Comprehension, Vocabulary, Fluency, Courage, Consistency, and future Subject Mastery families;
- filled evidence-backed state and unearned neutral placeholder;
- 24px header, 48px card, and 96px reward versions;
- monochrome and high-contrast variants;
- skill label and evidence sentence templates;
- no rarity tiers, trading motifs, price cues, or scarcity effects.

#### Buttons and controls

- primary, secondary, reward, and navigation visual masters;
- default, focus-visible, pressed, selected, disabled, loading, error states;
- child and adult density variants;
- 48px minimum target annotations;
- radio/choice tiles, switches, sliders, progress, fields, dialogs, toasts, and cards;
- reduced-motion and 200% text examples.

### Asset review gates

Before implementation, each asset package requires:

1. product-purpose review;
2. child-safety and emotional-boundary review;
3. literacy and cultural-sensitivity review where relevant;
4. accessibility review;
5. rights and provenance confirmation;
6. performance and responsive export review;
7. final naming and version registration.

## 7. Creative governance

- This Bible extends, not replaces, `docs/design-system.md`, `docs/companions.md`, `docs/ui-components.md`, and `docs/screen-redesign-roadmap.md`.
- If a visual idea conflicts with child safety, learning evidence, privacy, or accessibility, the idea changes—not the invariant.
- New worlds require a learning purpose before environment art begins.
- New rewards require evidence semantics before Gem art begins.
- New companion behavior requires a text equivalent and emotional-boundary review.
- Future implementation is staged in `docs/ui-redesign-roadmap.md`; no current runtime behavior changes in this phase.
