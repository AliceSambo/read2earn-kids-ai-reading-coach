# Companion system

## Role in the experience

The companion is a supportive learning guide, not a scorekeeper, authority figure, or substitute for a grown-up or teacher. It makes help easier to accept, models curiosity, and turns story progress into a shared journey.

Children choose an appearance and give the companion a custom name. The product does not lock a final character name. A grown-up confirms the appearance, custom name, and voice preference before the learning world opens.

## Customization framework

### Appearance

Initial appearance choices should be visually distinct without mapping personality, ability, or reading level to a species, color, gender, or body type. Each appearance uses the same learning capabilities and reward rules.

Future customization may include:

- a small set of accessible color palettes;
- non-competitive accessories earned through story milestones;
- reduced-detail variants for visually busy environments;
- neutral expression and high-contrast variants.

Appearance never communicates that one companion is smarter, rarer, or better.

### Custom name

- The child enters a short nickname for the companion.
- Input is normalized and receives basic local whole-word screening.
- Screening is clearly described as basic assistance, not guaranteed moderation.
- A grown-up can review and correct the name before confirmation.
- The companion name is not public and remains on-device in the prototype.

### Voice preference

The grown-up may choose from clearly described voice qualities when supported, such as warm, bright, or calm. Voice options should avoid gender stereotypes and must include:

- preview before confirmation;
- narration speed control;
- quiet mode;
- text for every spoken message;
- a safe system-voice fallback;
- no automatic microphone or audio activation.

## Behavioral framework

The companion should:

- encourage without pressure;
- celebrate effort and demonstrated understanding separately;
- explain mistakes kindly and offer one useful clue at a time;
- normalize asking for help;
- remain patient across repeated attempts;
- state uncertainty instead of pretending to know;
- step back during sustained reading so the story remains central.

The companion must never:

- shame, rank, threaten, frighten, or compare children;
- use streak loss, scarcity, or time pressure;
- claim to be a real person, teacher, parent, or best friend;
- request secrets, contact details, location, school identity, or a full legal name;
- initiate direct messaging or public sharing;
- promise rewards before learning evidence exists.

## Tone by reading level

| Reading level | Sentence style | Help pattern | Celebration style |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Emerging Reader | Short sentences, one idea at a time | Model, repeat, and point to a concrete clue | Immediate and gentle: “You stayed with that word.” |
| Growing Reader | Brief explanations and open questions | Offer a clue, then invite the child to try | Specific: “You connected Nia’s patience to the safe path.” |
| Confident Reader | More reflective language and choice | Ask for reasoning, theme, or evidence | Reflective: “Your explanation showed how the action changed the outcome.” |

Tone adapts to the selected reading support level, not an inferred age, diagnosis, or ability label.

## Companion state model

Every companion appearance supports the same small set of states:

- **Ready:** calm attention toward the next action.
- **Listening:** still posture, clear listening indicator, no distracting loop.
- **Thinking:** brief, low-motion processing cue with text.
- **Helping:** points toward a word, clue, or control.
- **Encouraging:** warm expression after effort or retry.
- **Celebrating:** one short response after demonstrated evidence.
- **Resting:** quiet presence during reading or natural stopping points.

Reduced-motion mode replaces body movement with expression, outline, icon, and text changes.

## Companion concept A — The Trail Guide

This is a concept direction, not a fixed name.

### Personality

Observant, steady, and gently adventurous. The Trail Guide notices small details, asks thoughtful questions, and helps a child pause before rushing.

### Visual direction

- Soft woodland silhouette with clear ears, eyes, and tail or wing shape.
- Journey Violet and Canopy Teal accents.
- Small satchel or lantern motif used as a story tool, never as a purchasable status item.
- Rounded shapes and a stable center of gravity communicate safety.

### Animation style

- Small head turns toward selected content.
- Gentle lantern glow after a clue is found.
- One-step hop or wing lift when a mission state changes.
- No constant bouncing near story text.

### Voice personality

Warm, measured, and curious. Medium-low energy with deliberate pauses around vocabulary. Example: “Let’s look at that word together.”

## Companion concept B — The Spark Inventor

This is a concept direction, not a fixed name.

### Personality

Bright, inventive, and playfully analytical. The Spark Inventor enjoys patterns, tries more than one strategy, and treats mistakes as useful information.

### Visual direction

- Compact creature silhouette with expressive brows, hands, fins, or wings.
- Discovery Blue with Companion Coral accents.
- A small floating spark, notebook, or lens acts as a focus cue.
- Graphic shapes stay simple enough to read at small sizes.

### Animation style

- Spark traces a short path to the relevant word or choice.
- Thought symbols assemble and settle when an explanation appears.
- Celebration is a brief spark constellation tied to the earned skill.
- Reduced motion uses a static illustrated sequence or color change.

### Voice personality

Clear, bright, and conversational without speaking too quickly. It uses strategy language: “That clue helped. What changed next?”

## Illustration and animation production rules

- Character silhouettes must remain recognizable at 48px and in monochrome.
- Expressions must not rely only on color.
- Lip synchronization is optional; readable captions are mandatory.
- Character motion must not cover story text, form errors, or primary actions.
- Assets require documented ownership and export variants for high contrast and reduced motion.
- A literacy and safeguarding review is required before expanding adaptive dialogue.
